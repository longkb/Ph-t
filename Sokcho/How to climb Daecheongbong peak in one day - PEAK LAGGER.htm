How to climb Daecheongbong peak in one day

November 13, 2016 Nikhil Leave a comment

The 1708 meter peak of Seoraksan National Park, Daecheongbong is the third tallest mountain in South Korea and offers more of a challenge than Jirisan orHallsan. The summit is tucked away in the southern end of the park and as such is not easy to access from the Sokcho entrance. As such, most climbers end up spending a night in Yangpok shelter before attempting the summit at dawn and then heading back north.

I did not have the luxury of time and so was forced to look at alternative routes. Below I will be talking about how you can actually climb the peak in a single day if you enter the national park from the south side.

Transport

First, rather than taking a bus to the national park – head towards Osaek instead. The bus towards Oseak leaves from the main bus terminal where you most likely arrived in Sokcho. The earliest bus leaves around 8am, which is the one I recommend taking in order to fit this mammoth hike into a single day. Make sure to tell the bus driver you intend to get off at the national park and he/she will signal to you when you arrive. Usually though, there are bound to be other hikers on the bus too and so you can often just tag behind someone carrying a pair of trekking poles.

The Route

As you can see above, I started from below at Osaek and headed up north past Seorak waterfalls to Daecheongbong peak. From here you continue onward toward Huiungak shelter and then have to decide between two options. Either one will take you back toward Bisondae and the northern entrance of the park.

Dragon-ridge Option

This is the more strenuous but also the more rewarding option. I would not recommend this route to be attempted in a single day from Osaek unless you were particularly fit and experienced. This route takes through the ups and downs of the famous dragon ridge of the national park before going back to Bisondae.

Cheonbuldong Valley Option

This was the route I took and the one I recommend if you are planning on climbing Daecheongbong peak in a single day. This route is much less strenuous and features a gentle steady decline past the gorgeous valley.

Ascent To Daecheongbong Peak

The hike up from Osaek takes 4-5 hours. It is a dreary climb up rocks and stairs and can be rather steep at a few points. There is a waterfall that marks the halfway point of the ascent. It took me roughly 4 hours to get up to the peak, at which point I was exhausted and starving!

Unfortunately for me, in spite of the good weather forecasts, there were clouds shrouding the top of the mountain and I couldn’t get any views of the national park at the summit.

I stopped here for a quick rest and scoffed down some lunch before trudging back down towards the northern end of the national park.

Descent to Yangpok Shelter

This was the most stunning part of the actual hike and took place atop this walkway on the mountain ridge.

Once again, Korean hiking paths reminded of what a pedestrian roller coaster would look like. The stairways sometimes steeply fall beyond a mountain precipice, which really gets the adrenaline pumping.

The route from the top to Yangpok is rather steep and strenuous. My stupid knee threatened to rip apart its menisci on a number of occasions so I caved in to its demands and slowed my pace down here.

To be honest, up and til this point, I had made good progress and even toyed with the idea of attempting the dragon ridge route. However with my feeble body letting my down, I was forced to progress a little more slowly than I would have liked.

The maps also severely underestimate the length of this part of the hike. A good third of the day was taken up trudging along from the peak to the Yangpok shelter.

Cheonbuldong Valley

Time was flying by the time  I got to Yangpok – it was already close to 3pm! The sun was due to set around 6 and I did not want to get stranded on the mountain after dark. I picked up my pace a little but that proved to a difficult job due to how stunning the surrounding valley was.

 

I had to stop and pause to take photos on numerous occasions. Despite this, I managed to make it to Bisondae with about an hour to spare from the sunset. Rather than sensibly going home, I decided to pay a visit to the nearby Geumggangul cave.

Geumggangul Cave

By this point I was tired and fatigued, but also wanted to make the most out of the day. As such, I limped up a ridiculous amount of stairs and found myself resting in a stunning temple alcove. The panic of darkness somewhat undermined my ability to relax and take in the tranquillity of the location.

After realising that the real world was not minecraft, I decided that hiking after sunset would not be so catastrophic and took a sensible pace back down the stairs but I did contemplate throwing myself down them on a couple of occasions.

Bisondae

I managed to reach Bisondae just as the light was starting to fade. Luckily I brought a head torch but regrettably forgot insect repellent! The mosquitos started to come out but I finished the hike before their numbers became truly frightening. The route back to the national park from Bisondae is a flat paved path that was not very difficult to negotiate.

Back to Sokcho

Buses leave from the park back to Sokcho all the way up till 9 pm so missing them is not really a worry. From the main park entrance, I caught the 7 pm bus and headed back into town.

The whole day was very long and involved 13 hours of hiking, but in the end it was a very worthwhile way to see so much of the national park.
